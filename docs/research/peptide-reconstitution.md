# What is Peptide Reconstitution

Reconstitution refers to the process of dissolving a lyophilized (freeze-dried) peptide in a laboratory solvent to create a solution suitable for research applications.

Lyophilized peptides are typically shipped in powdered form to maintain stability during storage and transport.

---

# Common Laboratory Solvents

Researchers may use various laboratory-grade solvents depending on experimental design.

Commonly referenced solvents include:

- sterile water
- bacteriostatic water
- dilute acetic acid solutions
- dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)

The choice of solvent depends on peptide properties such as solubility and stability.

---

# Best Practices for Reconstitution

Typical laboratory practices include:

- allowing the vial to reach room temperature before opening
- adding solvent slowly to the vial
- gently swirling the solution to dissolve the peptide
- avoiding vigorous shaking

Researchers often divide reconstituted solutions into **aliquots** to minimize freeze–thaw cycles.

---

# Storage After Reconstitution

Once dissolved, peptide solutions are commonly stored under refrigerated or frozen conditions depending on experimental requirements.

Minimizing exposure to heat, light, and repeated freeze–thaw cycles may help maintain stability.

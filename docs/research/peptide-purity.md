# What is Peptide Purity

Peptide purity refers to the proportion of the target peptide present in a sample relative to impurities or byproducts generated during synthesis.

High purity peptides are important for laboratory research because impurities may influence experimental results or reproducibility.

---

# Methods Used to Evaluate Purity

Researchers commonly evaluate peptide purity using analytical techniques such as:

- High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)
- Mass Spectrometry (MS)
- Analytical chromatography methods

These techniques allow researchers to confirm both peptide identity and purity.

---

# High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

HPLC is widely used to measure peptide purity.

During analysis:

- the peptide sample passes through a chromatographic column
- compounds separate based on chemical interactions
- a chromatogram displays peaks representing compounds in the sample

The main peak typically corresponds to the target peptide.

Purity is often expressed as the percentage of the total chromatographic area represented by the primary peak.

---

# Mass Spectrometry (MS)

Mass spectrometry is used to confirm the molecular weight of a peptide.

This technique allows researchers to verify that the synthesized peptide corresponds to the expected molecular structure.

---

# Factors Affecting Purity

Peptide purity may be influenced by several factors including:

- synthesis methods
- incomplete reactions during peptide assembly
- oxidation or degradation
- handling and storage conditions

---

# Laboratory Considerations

Researchers may select peptide purity levels based on experimental needs.

Higher purity peptides are often preferred for sensitive laboratory experiments requiring reproducible results.
